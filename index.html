Introduction

    Why is Letcher County an unsustainable location for a new federal prison?

    In creating this website, we wanted to address the issues, both environmental and social, that are prosed by the implementation of the Letcher County Prison Proposal, first introduced in 2006. While some argue that the construction of a federal prison in this small corner of Eastern Kentucky will create jobs and foster opportunity, we hope to dispel these myths by explaining the multitude of harmful impacts this would cause.

Coal, Prisons, and Appalachia

    How are the coal industry and the incarceration system related? And why are they so present in Appalachia?

  • According to Judah Schept in his book Coal, Cages, Crisis: “mass incarceration would not be possible, of course, without the prisons in which people are incarcerated. And Central Appalachia is home to many prisons.”

  • The phenomenon he labeled as “carceral social reproduction”-- the dependency on the construction of prisons to alleviate the economic crises associated with unemployment, degrading or nonexistent infrastructure, and the collapse of health, education, and revenue generating systems— allows us to connect eastern Kentucky, an area hosting 5 federal prisons, to the carceral state. In fact, at Kentucky’s current incarceration rate, the entire state will be imprisoned in 113 years.

  • Extraction has been a major theme throughout the history of Kentucky, especially in regards to the coal industry, as it refers to removing the value from one area and exporting it somewhere else. In the case of Appalachia, this occurs exclusively to the benefit of those who do not live in the region, and harms the livelihoods of residents who are forced to live next to the sites of extraction.

    • What does this mean?

  • Ever since Kentuckians in Appalachia were pushed off of their family farms and into the coal mines, their lives have been in the hands of an extractive industry. The departure of the coal industry from Appalachia left communities reeling for ways to generate revenue. This is part of the reason we see so many “undesirable” land uses across the region. This coupled with the “Tough on Crime” policies from the mid to late 1900s created a system that extracted people from urban settings, and “deposited” them in the rural prisons of Appalachia.

  • Therefore, Appalachia has been rendered as a “national sacrifice zone.” It has become a region of planned obsolescence… meaning that the presence of economic devastation both painted Appalachia as a wasteland AND justified the continuation of its polluting.

    • How many more federal prisons does Appalachia really need?

  • Well, none. Studies show that the national incarceration rate is gradually going down AND that out of the 5 Kentucky counties that already have federal prisons, all of them are still considered economically distressed per the Appalachian Regional Commission Criteria.

Why Letcher County?

Similar to many counties in the Southeastern region of Kentucky, Letcher County was a prime location for mountaintop removal coal mining. Mountains all over Appalachia are blown up to get the coal out all while maximizing profit. What’s left behind is a flat expanse of toxic, barren land, and a community that scarcely sees said profit. According to Hal Rogers, the region’s Congressional Representative, the solution is, yet another, federal prison. Furthermore, as stated by SCRMA, the Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act of 1977, the mined land needs only to be turned to a “higher use,” and a revenue-generating incarceration facility somehow fits that bill.

Background: According to the U.S. Census Bureau Data circa 2024, Letcher County is home to approximately 20,000 individuals: 97.6% of whom are white, 25% of residents are considered impoverished, and among them all there persists a 40% employment rate. While these statistics portray Letcher County as a stereotypical homogenous and in-need Appalachian community, they negate so much of the “why?” and therefore rob residents of the “what could be?” They gloss over the fact that at the time of the prison proposal, locals “hunted, foraged, rehabilitated injured birds, held family reunions, gathered for religious retreats, organized and held music festivals,” and more (Schept, 2022). They try to divert from the fact that “despite representations as a remote hinterland, Central Appalachia is in fact part of truly global flows: of capital, commodities, technologies, products, and people,” (Schept, 2022).

How Mountaintop Removal Affects Human Health and the Environment

    Mountaintop removal mining is a form of surface mining in which the top of a mountain is blasted away to access coal seams. This process raises concerns for both human health and the environment, especially in Central Appalachia.

    Effects on Human Health
  • Research from the National Toxicology Program shows that people who are exposed or live near a mountaintop removal site are exposed to pollutants through water, air, and soil. Studies by the NTP also reveal that there is a link to these exposures to cardiopulmonary disease, higher mortality rates, and birth defects. Such exposures include heavy metals, toxic chemicals, and other particulate matter which can negatively affect or harm the body. Thus, evidence suggests that it is biologically plausible that living near a mountaintop removal mining operation harms human health.

    • Effects on the Environment
  • Mountaintop removal has a widespread range of environmental impacts, especially on ecosystems and water quality/systems. According to research conducted by the United States Forest Service, this form of mining (mountaintop removal) alters land structure and increases erosion which often times prevents a full forest recovery from occurring. As a result, forest ecosystems are left less stable vegetation which results in reduced biodiversity. Mining waste also frequently buries nearby streams and contaminates water with harmful substances. Long term damage to aquatic systems and degraded water quality are also environmental changes that result from this form of mining (mountaintop removal) which can persist long after mining has stopped and effects can be long lasting.

How Prison Building Affects Human Health and the Environment

General: Prison Buildings, Health, and Environment Prisons are often built on cheap or environmentally damaged land. They also require lots of water, energy, and resources, in addition to the factors involved in contruction. On top of these impacts, they generate waste and air pollution, and incarcerated people are unable to avoid these unsafe conditions.

Letcher County Impacts The proposed prison in Letcher County is planned on a former mountaintop removal site. It's proximity to natural water sources increases the risk of contamination and pollution from harmful runoff. In addition to this, the prison is projected to bring limited long-term benefits, and it is largely opposed by the cmmunity, including groups like Building Community Not Prisons.

Community Voices

In an interview with lifelong Letcher County Resident Dr. Artie Ann Bates, she gives valuable insights into the impact that the contruction of a prison would have on the community and ecosystem:

"The proposal is preposterous... it would change the complexion of our rural community, and cause a lot of environmnetal damage."

"The site boasts no supporting infrastructure, no access to public transportation, is 100 miles away from the nearest interestate, and can only be reached via a two-lane road. After excavating the site, only $40 million of the allocated $506 million would be left for construction."

"According to the EIS of 20224, the prison would be difficult to staff considering there are 90 staff vacancies...furthermore, only 16% of Letcher county residents would eligible to work there."

"More attention needs to paid to the incarceration system in the first place... less money should be going to keeping people locked up, and more should be diverted to welfare programs that support individuals at a high risk of getting locked up in the first place. Rehabilitation and reentry would be nearly impossible in the mountains of Letcher County, and such a location is simply unfair to all incarcerated individuals."

"After decades of resource extraction, ecotourism is beginning to bloom in Letcher County. As a biodiversity hotspot, the mountains of Appalachia are not meant to be flattened to confine people."
